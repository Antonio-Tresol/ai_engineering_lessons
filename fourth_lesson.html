Evaluating Modern AI Systems

A study guide for AI Engineering Interns based on Chapter 4 of "AI Engineering".

The Core Philosophy

The single biggest bottleneck to AI adoption is reliable evaluation.

We will adopt the principle of Evaluation-Driven Development.

This means defining how you will measure success before you start building. An application that is deployed but cannot be evaluated is a liabilityâ€”it costs resources without providing knowable value.

"In AI engineering, evaluation-driven development means defining evaluation criteria before building."

— Huyen (2025, p. 160)

The Four Pillars of Evaluation

A comprehensive evaluation looks beyond simple accuracy.

1. Domain-Specific Capability

Does the model understand the subject matter (e.g., code, law)?

2. Generation Capability

How good is the quality of the open-ended output (e.g., factual, safe)?

3. Instruction-Following

Can the model adhere to specific constraints and formats?

4. Cost and Latency

Is the model fast and affordable enough for the use case?

Step 1: The Build vs. Buy Decision

This is your first, most critical filter. Should you self-host an open-source model or use a commercial API?

  • Data Privacy: Can data leave your network? Self-hosting provides maximum privacy.
  • Performance: The absolute SOTA models are often proprietary APIs, but the gap is closing.
  • Functionality & Control: Need to modify architecture or guarantee version stability? Self-hosting offers more control.
  • Cost at Scale: APIs are pay-per-use; self-hosting has high upfront engineering costs but can be cheaper at massive scale.
  • Data Lineage & IP Risk: Who is liable if the model was trained on copyrighted data? Commercial contracts may offer indemnification.

"A 2024 study by a16z shows two key reasons that enterprises care about open source models are control and customizability..."

— Huyen (2025, p. 189)

Step 2: Navigate Public Benchmarks

Use leaderboards to create a shortlist of promising models.

Their purpose is to give you a directional sense of a model's general capabilities in areas like reasoning (MMLU), math (GSM-8K), or coding (HumanEval).

CRITICAL CAVEAT: Data Contamination

"Data contamination happens when a model was trained on the same data itâ€™s evaluated on... causing it to achieve higher evaluation scores than it should" (Huyen, 2025, p. 197). Never trust public benchmarks as the sole source of truth.

Step 3: Design Your Custom Pipeline

This is where real engineering happens. Build a reliable, application-specific evaluation process.

1. Create a Clear Guideline

  • Define what a "good" response is, not just a "correct" one.
  • Create detailed scoring rubrics with concrete examples.
  • Tie technical metrics (e.g., accuracy) to business metrics (e.g., time saved).

2. Define Methods & Data

  • Curate an evaluation set from real user data.
  • Use AI judges, specialized classifiers, or human experts.
  • Slice your data to test performance on critical subsets (e.g., high-value users, edge cases). This helps avoid Simpson's Paradox.

"A correct response is not always a good response. For example, for their AI-powered Job Assessment application, the response 'You are a terrible fit' might be correct but not helpful, thus making it a bad response."

— Huyen (2025, p. 202)

Key Takeaways

  • Adopt Evaluation-Driven Development. Define success before you build.
  • The Build vs. Buy decision is a strategic one that depends on privacy, control, and cost.
  • Use public benchmarks for initial filtering but do not trust them blindly due to data contamination.
  • The reliability of your AI application is determined by the quality of your custom evaluation pipeline. This is your source of truth.

"Not having a reliable evaluation pipeline is one of the biggest blocks to AI adoption."

— Huyen (2025, p. 208)
